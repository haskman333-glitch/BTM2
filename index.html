Dyson’s AirPulse i.d. Cleans Teeth With Air—and Costs $778

Dyson’s first electric toothbrush eliminates bristles in favour of a miniature high-speed compressor, shaped air channels, six months of battery life and live cleaning guidance through MyDyson.

The most important part of the AirPulse i.d. is the part that is missing. There is no oscillating brush head, no bundle of nylon filaments and no replaceable tip. Dyson has instead built the machine around a pair of carefully shaped air outlets designed to clean the surface of each tooth without making direct contact.

The result is recognisably a toothbrush, but one organised around the internal logic of an airflow appliance. Air enters through the rear grille, passes through a compact motor-driven compression system, travels along the tapered neck and exits through two narrow oval channels at the top.

That architecture changes almost every familiar element of an electric toothbrush: how it cleans, how it warns the user, how its battery is charged, how modes are selected and even which parts need replacing.

AirPulse i.d. does not move a brush against the tooth. It shapes, compresses and directs the air itself.

Cleaning With Compressed Air

The concept begins with an unusually simple claim: a toothbrush does not necessarily need bristles if its airflow can be made concentrated, consistent and controllable enough to disturb plaque and move debris away from the enamel.

Inside the head, a small sequence of compression surfaces reduces the available air path before dividing it between the two outlets. The geometry accelerates the air and gives each stream a narrow, controlled shape rather than allowing it to leave the head as a broad, weak gust.

The two elongated outlets are intended to overlap slightly. One stream focuses on the visible surface of the tooth while the second reaches closer to the gumline and the spaces around neighbouring teeth.

Unlike a conventional toothbrush, the head is not meant to be pressed against the enamel. Dyson specifies a small working gap between the outlet and the tooth. At that distance, the air has enough space to form correctly before reaching the surface.

Pressing the head directly against the tooth obstructs the outlets and reduces cleaning performance. That is why AirPulse i.d. treats excessive contact as an error rather than simply as excessive brushing pressure.

A Body Shaped Around One Continuous Air Path

The product is visually simple because the upper nozzle, the internal channel and the handle have been designed as one connected structure.

AirPulse i.d. stands 230 millimetres tall. Based on the proportions of the full product render, the widest copper shoulder is approximately 27 millimetres across, while the main purple grip is approximately 23 millimetres in diameter.

The lower handle contains the battery, motor, electronics and display. Above it, the copper shoulder creates additional internal volume for the compression geometry before the structure narrows into the long white or graphite neck.

The cleaning tip is made from solid moulded plastic and is integrated permanently into the upper body. Because it contains no bristles, there is no brush head to wear down, detach or replace every few months.

The only major removable component is the battery module at the bottom of the handle. It separates from the main body when charging is eventually required.

Very Low Torque, Very High Speed

A conventional electric toothbrush must repeatedly accelerate and reverse a brush head while that head is being pressed against the teeth. That requires usable torque and creates a constantly changing mechanical load.

AirPulse i.d. uses a different operating profile. Its compact digital motor drives a small fan blade continuously in one direction. The required torque is comparatively low, allowing the motor to be optimised around a narrow and highly efficient high-rpm operating range.

The fan creates the initial airflow. The shoulder and neck then compress and guide it before the twin nozzle geometry produces the final cleaning streams.

Six Months Between Charges

The efficiency of the low-load fan system gives AirPulse i.d. a claimed active runtime of 30 hours. That figure refers to the time spent actually operating, rather than standby time.

At three three-minute sessions per day, daily motor use totals nine minutes. Thirty hours therefore provides approximately 200 days of operation—slightly more than six months.

Dyson has omitted an induction stand entirely. When the battery is finally depleted, the lower module detaches from the handle and is placed into a dedicated external charger.

A full charge takes 10 hours. The slow charging cycle is intended to reduce heat and preserve the long-term capacity of a battery that may only need to be charged twice per year.

The dedicated charger is not included with the toothbrush. It is sold separately for $215.

One Button, One Circular Display

The physical power button handles starting and stopping. Everything else is controlled through the circular touch display positioned directly above it.

Motion sensors detect movement and wake the otherwise dark circular display automatically. When the handle is placed back down, the display returns to black.

Horizontal gestures move between Clean, Soft Clean and Aqua Floss before the motor is started. Pressing the single physical button starts the motor and locks the selected programme so an accidental touch cannot change the mode during cleaning.

Every programme lasts three minutes. A short vibration is produced every 45 seconds, creating four equal cleaning sections before the motor stops automatically.

Pressure and Battery Warnings

Move the nozzle away

When the head is pressed directly against the tooth, pressure and obstruction sensors recognise that the airflow path has been compromised. The handle produces a warning vibration pattern and the display flashes a strong red alert symbol.

Battery depleted

A yellow empty-battery symbol appears when the remaining charge can no longer support a full cycle. Pressing the start button produces a short vibration, while the yellow symbol flashes at increased brightness for three seconds. The motor remains off.

Three Cleaning Programmes

Clean

The standard daily programme uses the full dry-air output. A short vibration is produced every 45 seconds, dividing the three-minute cleaning cycle into four equal sections.

Soft Clean

Soft Clean reduces the airflow pressure to limit irritation around sensitive gums. The programme keeps the same three-minute structure and the same four 45-second guidance intervals.

Aqua Floss

Aqua Floss uses the optional rear-mounted water reservoir. The attachment covers the normal intake grille and engages a dedicated sealed wet-mode connection, converting the twin outlet into a pulsed water-flossing system.

MyDyson Shows What You Missed

AirPulse i.d. connects to the MyDyson app to display the actual remaining charge of the removable battery rather than reducing it to a basic three-level icon.

During cleaning, the app presents a live map of the mouth. Areas move from red to yellow and finally to green as the system records sufficient coverage.

The map is designed to show which teeth remain dirty, which have been cleaned only partially and which have received the complete cleaning cycle.

When the Aqua Floss attachment is connected, an additional menu appears inside the same AirPulse product page. It displays the remaining water level and tracks flossing coverage separately from dry-air cleaning.

Then There Is the Price

At $778 for the handle alone, AirPulse i.d. sits at the very top of the consumer electric-toothbrush market. The cost does not include the dedicated battery charger, which adds another $215.

The optional Aqua Floss attachment costs $293. A complete system therefore reaches $1,286.

The complete proposition follows a familiar Dyson pattern: take a simple domestic object, identify the airflow problem hidden inside it, replace the conventional mechanism with an unusually elaborate motor and channel system, then build a premium ecosystem around the result.
